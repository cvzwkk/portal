In quantum mechanics, systems like electrons, photons, or atoms can exist in a **superposition** of multiple states at the same time. This is the core "quantum" behavior: the particle isn't in one definite state (like a classical object) but in a blend of possibilities, described by a wave function (ψ). Interference effects, entanglement, and probabilities arise from this.

The question seems to ask: **Does an intentional interaction or measurement ("intentional inst" — I'm interpreting this as intentional instrument/measurement/observation) turn the system non-quantum (classical-like), or does it stay quantum?** Here's the clear answer, step by step:

### 1. What "intentional" measurement actually does
- A measurement isn't about "intention" in a magical or conscious sense. It is a **physical interaction** between the quantum system and a measuring device (the "instrument").
- The device is usually macroscopic and classical (e.g., a detector, a screen, or even air molecules in the environment).
- When the system interacts with the instrument, the quantum superposition **collapses** (in the standard Copenhagen interpretation) or **decoheres** (in modern understanding).

Result:  
The system stops behaving as a pure quantum superposition for the measured property. It jumps to **one definite outcome** (an eigenstate). This is what we call "becoming non-quantum" or classical-like for that observable.

Example:  
An electron in superposition of spin-up and spin-down (quantum).  
You intentionally measure its spin with a magnet detector → it instantly gives either up or down (non-quantum definite result). You can no longer see interference from the superposition.

Mathematically (in Dirac notation):  
Before measurement:  
$$\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha |\uparrow\rangle + \beta |\downarrow\rangle \]$$  
(quantum superposition, probabilities |α|² and |β|²)  

After measurement:  
The wave function "collapses" to either  
$$\[ |\psi\rangle = |\uparrow\rangle \quad \text{or} \quad |\psi\rangle = |\downarrow\rangle \]$$  
No more superposition for spin.

### 2. Does it *keep* being quantum?
- **No, not in superposition for the measured observable.** The quantum weirdness (superposition, interference) is lost for that specific property.
- **Yes, the underlying physics is still quantum.** The system is always governed by quantum rules. The collapsed state is still a valid quantum state — just no longer a superposition of the thing you measured. If you measure a different property later, new quantum behavior can appear.
- The system does **not** stay in its original quantum superposition once measured. It cannot "keep being quantum" in the sense of preserving the original superposition after intentional interaction.

### 3. How it happens (the "how")
- **Standard view (Copenhagen interpretation):** Measurement is a special postulate. The wave function collapses instantly and irreversibly when the system interacts with the classical instrument. "Intentional" doesn't matter — any interaction that extracts information does it. Consciousness or human intention plays no special role.
- **Modern view (decoherence + many-worlds or consistent histories):** No mysterious collapse. The quantum system entangles with the huge number of degrees of freedom in the measuring device + environment. The superposition becomes "smeared out" across the environment so fast that interference becomes unobservable. It *looks* classical, but everything is still quantum underneath. Again, intention is irrelevant — it's pure physics.

In both cases:  
Intentional measurement/instrument → system **becomes effectively non-quantum** (definite, classical outcome) for the measured property.

### 4. What if there is *no* intentional measurement?
- The system stays fully quantum: superposition, entanglement, interference all preserved (until natural decoherence from the environment happens anyway).
- Example: Isolated quantum computer qubits can stay in superposition for a while if protected from any interaction.

### Key takeaway
An **intentional instrument/measurement** does **not** keep the system in its original quantum superposition — it forces the system into a non-quantum (definite/classical) behavior for that observable. This is not because of "intention" magically, but because of unavoidable physical interaction. Quantum mechanics has no special rule for "intentional vs accidental"; any coupling to the outside world does the job.
