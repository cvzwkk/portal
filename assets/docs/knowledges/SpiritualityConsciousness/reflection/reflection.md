**Advaita Vedanta** (literally "not-two" or "non-secondness") is the premier non-dual school of Hindu philosophy, systematized by **Adi Shankaracharya** (c. 788–820 CE). It builds on the Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Bhagavad Gita (the *Prasthanatrayi*). ### Core Metaphysics

- **Brahman**: The absolute, non-dual reality — pure consciousness (*sat-chit-ananda*: existence-consciousness-bliss), formless, unchanging, infinite, without attributes (*nirguna*). It is the substrate of all existence, not a personal creator God in the Abrahamic sense, though often equated with "God" at the relative level (*saguna Brahman* or Ishvara).
- **Atman**: The innermost self or pure awareness in each being. The great teaching (*mahavakya*) is **Tat Tvam Asi** ("Thou art That") — Atman *is* Brahman. The sense of separate individuality is ignorance (*avidya*).
- **Maya**: The power of appearance or illusion. The world and individual egos appear real from the empirical standpoint (*vyavaharika*) but are sublated (revealed as unreal) upon realization (*paramarthika* — absolute standpoint). Like a rope mistaken for a snake in dim light.
- **Two Levels of Reality**: Empirical (world, actions, devotion have validity for daily life) and absolute (only Brahman). This resolves apparent contradictions without strict nihilism.

Liberation (*moksha*) is not attaining something new but **removing ignorance** (*avidya*) through knowledge (*jnana*), realizing one's ever-free nature.

**Free Will Paradox**: In ultimate terms, there is no separate "doer" — the egoic self that chooses is part of Maya. **Dogmas and Manipulation**: Advaita prioritizes direct realization over ritual or blind faith. Shankaracharya critiqued excessive externalism. While traditions and teachers exist, the path is ultimately inward — bypassing institutional control. This resonates with mystical critiques in Sufism or Christian apophatic theology.

**Humanity's Purpose**: Imposed purpose (worship, obedience) is relative. The "true" purpose is self-realization — awakening to non-duality. This naturally yields compassion, harmony, and ethical living (as all beings are Brahman). Progress and peace emerge from reduced ego-identification rather than forced rules. It offers an emergent, experiential meaning compatible with secular inquiry.

### Key Practices: Jnana Yoga and Self-Inquiry

Advaita is primarily the path of knowledge, though it integrates preparatory disciplines.

**Core Method — Atma Vichara (Self-Inquiry)**, famously emphasized by Ramana Maharshi:
1. Turn attention inward with the question: **"Who am I?"** (or "To whom do these thoughts arise?").
2. Observe the "I"-thought or sense of self. Trace it to its source.
3. Negate what you are not (*neti-neti*): "I am not the body, not the senses, not the thoughts, not the mind, not the ego."
4. Rest in the pure awareness that remains — the witness beyond objects. This is not creating a state but recognizing what is always present.

- **Sravana** (hearing/studying scriptures and teachings).
- **Manana** (reflecting, removing doubts).
- **Nididhyasana** (deep contemplation, abiding in the truth).

These are direct and experiential, aligning with the mystical practices we discussed earlier.
